# Tiredness vs Fatigue

## What it is
Tiredness and fatigue describe overlapping but distinct presentations. Tiredness is a general, near-universal symptom; fatigue is the persistent, long-term state that arises when tiredness is not resolved by sleep (see [Mina Shahnawaz – Tiredness](../sources/mina-shahnawaz-tiredness.md)).

## Description
### Definitions
- **Tiredness:** a general symptom that almost everyone experiences (see [source](../sources/mina-shahnawaz-tiredness.md)).
- **Fatigue:** the long-term effect of tiredness that is not resolved by sleep (see [source](../sources/mina-shahnawaz-tiredness.md)).

### Epidemiology in primary care
- Roughly 7% of all GP consultations are due to fatigue (see [source](../sources/mina-shahnawaz-tiredness.md)).
- To the nearest 10%, about 66% of patients presenting with fatigue receive a diagnosis (see [source](../sources/mina-shahnawaz-tiredness.md)).
- Fewer than 10% of patients presenting with fatigue have a condition detected on blood tests (see [source](../sources/mina-shahnawaz-tiredness.md)), underscoring that history is often more informative than early bloods.

### Management of non-pathological tiredness
- Improve: healthy diet, regular exercise, consistent sleep timing (aim 6–9 hours), winding down before bed, and a quiet/dark/warm sleep environment (see [source](../sources/mina-shahnawaz-tiredness.md)).
- Avoid: smoking, excess alcohol, and caffeine/eating/exercise/screens in the hours before sleep (see [source](../sources/mina-shahnawaz-tiredness.md)).

## Open questions
- The distinction between "tiredness" and "fatigue" here is a working clinical definition; whether the course requires a stricter physiological boundary is unconfirmed.
