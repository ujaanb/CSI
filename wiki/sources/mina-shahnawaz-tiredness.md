## Summary
A CSI case study built around a patient ("Mina Shahnawaz") presenting with tiredness. It is organised as Pre-Reading, Main Session, and Post-Session flashcards. It distinguishes tiredness from fatigue, gives epidemiology of fatigue in primary care, and lists common causes and a broad differential diagnosis. It then focuses on primary hypothyroidism, explaining the hypothalamic–pituitary–thyroid (HPT) axis and why primary hypothyroidism produces high TSH with low T3/T4. It details the immune sequence in Hashimoto's disease (anti-TPO autoimmunity) and recaps immune tolerance concepts from Phase 1A (central and peripheral tolerance, AIRE, positive/negative selection, anergy, ignorance, Tregs, affinity maturation). Post-Session material lists causes and symptoms of hypothyroidism and notes the drugs lithium and amiodarone.

## Key facts
- Tiredness is a near-universal general symptom; fatigue is the long-term effect of tiredness that is not resolved by sleep.
- Roughly 7% of all GP consultations are due to fatigue.
- ~66% (to the nearest 10%) of patients presenting to a GP with fatigue have a diagnosis made.
- <10% of patients presenting with fatigue have a condition detected on blood tests.
- Common causes of tiredness: insufficient/poor sleep (insomnia), unhealthy lifestyle, stress/depression/life challenges, hormonal changes (puberty, pregnancy, menopause), illnesses (glandular fever, COVID-19), and medical treatments (e.g. chemotherapy).
- Conditions that may cause tiredness/fatigue include: anaemia (incl. iron deficiency), sleep apnoea, hypothyroidism, coeliac disease, chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS), diabetes, glandular fever, depression, restless legs syndrome, anxiety, malignancy, vitamin D deficiency, heart failure, infection, and lupus.
- Sleep hygiene advice to improve fatigue: healthy diet, regular exercise, consistent sleep timing (aim 6–9 hours), relaxing before bed, and a quiet/dark/warm sleep environment. Avoid smoking, excess alcohol, and caffeine/eating/exercise/screens close to bedtime.
- Differential diagnosis for fatigue relies on asking about symptom frequency and severity, plus system-review questions and tests (e.g. bloods) to rule conditions in or out.
- Primary hypothyroidism produces high TSH with low T3/T4 because the failing thyroid cannot generate the negative feedback (via T3/T4) that would normally suppress TRH/TSH.
- In Hashimoto's disease, thyroid peroxidase (TPO) is recognised as foreign, triggering an adaptive immune response that produces anti-TPO antibodies which attack the thyroid.
- Central tolerance acts on B/T cells before release into circulation; peripheral tolerance acts after release.
- AIRE (Autoimmune Regulator) transcription factors let the thymus express peripheral tissue antigens so self-reactive T cells can be identified and destroyed; AIRE mutations cause multi-organ autoimmunity.
- Causes of hypothyroidism include autoimmune thyroid disease (e.g. Hashimoto's), radioactive iodine treatment, antithyroid drugs, lithium, amiodarone, excess iodine, pituitary malfunction, head/neck radiation, and congenital hypothyroidism.
- Lithium is commonly used to treat some mental health problems; amiodarone is commonly used to treat some heart problems.
- Symptoms of hypothyroidism include fatigue, cold intolerance, dry/coarse skin, dry/thinning hair, hoarse voice, constipation, muscle weakness/cramps, carpal tunnel syndrome, heavier/longer periods, low libido, weight gain, puffy face, slowed speech/movement/thought, low mood, memory/concentration problems, bradycardia, raised blood pressure, and raised cholesterol.
