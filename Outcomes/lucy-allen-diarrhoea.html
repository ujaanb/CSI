1 Definitions & Classification

Pre-Session — what diarrhoea is and how it is classified.

Define diarrhoea

  • Three or more loose or liquid stools per 24 hours, and/or
  • Stools more frequent than normal for the individual (lasting <14 days), and/or
  • Stool weight greater than 200 g/day

Classification by duration

TypeDuration
Acute≤ 14 days
Persistent> 14 days
Chronic> 4 weeks

What causes diarrhoea?

  • Occurs when various factors interfere with the normal processes of water reabsorption or secretion
  • Characterised by decreased reabsorption or increased secretion of fluid and electrolytes, or increased bowel motility

The two main categories

  • Inflammatory
  • Non-inflammatory

2 GI Fluid Handling

How much fluid enters the GI tract daily, and its sources?

  • ~10 litres per day
  • Ingested food and drink (~2 L), salivary glands (~1.5 L), stomach (~2.5 L — HCl, mucus), pancreas (~1.5 L), bile ducts (~0.5 L), duodenum (~1 L — mucus, bicarbonate, enzymes)

Fluid reabsorption

  • Water and electrolytes initially secreted into the digestive system are actively transported back into the bloodstream through the intestinal wall ("re-absorbed" from the gut lumen)
  • Purpose: retain most of the fluid consumed and prevent excessive fluid loss in stool
  • Main site: the small intestine
  • ~99% of the fluid (~9.9 L) is reabsorbed; ~0.1 L is excreted in faeces daily
  • The process is passive, driven by osmotic gradients: water moves by osmosis following the absorption of solutes like Na⁺, Cl⁻, glucose, and amino acids

3 Inflammatory Diarrhoea

What is inflammatory diarrhoea?

A form of diarrhoea that indicates the presence of an inflammatory process.

Causes and clinical features

  • Causes: bacterial infection, viral infection, parasitic infection, bowel ischaemia, radiation injury, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)
  • Clinical features: mucoid/bloody stool, tenesmus, fever, severe crampy abdominal pain
  • Tenesmus: the feeling that you need a bowel movement even though your bowels are empty

Infectious inflammatory diarrhoea and volume depletion

  • Induced by bacterial, viral or parasitic infection
  • Usually small in volume with frequent bowel movements
  • Volume depletion: a reduction in extracellular fluid (ECF) volume when salt and fluid losses exceed intake on a sustained basis
  • Does not usually cause volume depletion in adults, but may in children or older adults
  • Most common US causes: bacterial (Campylobacter, Salmonella, Shigella, E. coli, C. difficile). Viruses commonly affect children in day care; protozoa/parasites in developing countries

Identifying it clinically; histology

  • Stool may show leukocytes; tests for faecal occult blood may be positive
  • Note The faecal leukocyte test has a high false-negative rate (low sensitivity), but a positive test is very informative — leukocytes indicate bowel inflammation
  • GI histology is abnormal in inflammatory diarrhoea

4 Non-inflammatory Diarrhoea

What is it, and its clinical features?

  • A form of diarrhoea indicating the absence of an inflammatory process
  • Usually watery, large-volume, frequent stool (>10–20 per day); volume depletion is possible due to high volume/frequency
  • Clinical features: no tenesmus, no blood in stool, no fever, no faecal leukocytes
  • GI architecture is preserved histologically
  • Two types: secretory and osmotic

Secretory diarrhoea

  • Altered transport of ions across the mucosa (esp. small intestine) → increased secretion and decreased absorption of fluids/electrolytes
  • Does not decrease by fasting
  • Causes: enterotoxins (Vibrio cholerae, Staph aureus, enterotoxigenic E. coli, possibly HIV and rotavirus), hormonal agents (VIP, small-cell lung cancer, neuroblastoma), laxative use, intestinal resection, bile salts, fatty acids. Also chronic causes: coeliac sprue, collagenous colitis, hyperthyroidism, carcinoid tumours

Osmotic diarrhoea

  • Caused by poorly reabsorbed substances in the intestines drawing excess fluid into the bowel lumen → watery stools
  • Improves or stops with fasting
  • Stool volume relatively small (vs secretory)
  • Caused by unabsorbed/poorly absorbed solutes (magnesium, sorbitol, mannitol)
  • Test: stool electrolytes show an increased osmotic gap (>50); stool is always isosmotic (260–290 mOsm/L) with serum

The osmotic gap; maldigestion vs malabsorption

  • Osmotic gap: the difference between the measured osmolality of a fluid and the calculated osmolality from known solute concentrations (sodium, potassium, urea, glucose)
  • Maldigestion: impaired digestion of nutrients within the intestinal lumen at the brush border membrane → poorly absorbed solute. Causes: pancreatic exocrine insufficiency, lactase deficiency
  • Malabsorption: impaired absorption of nutrients → poorly absorbed solute. Causes: small bowel bacterial overgrowth, mesenteric ischaemia, post bowel resection (short bowel syndrome), mucosal disease (coeliac disease)

5 Ion Transport & Water Movement

Main Session — the cellular basis of secretion and reabsorption.

Daily water figures and transport routes

  • Excreted in faeces: 100–200 ml/day; consumed: ~1–2 L/day; secreted into GI tract: ~6–7 L/day
  • Transcellular transport: solutes travel through a cell
  • Paracellular transport: solutes travel around a cell, through adjacent gap junctions

Water secretion into the GI tract

  • Apical Cl⁻ ion channels allow Cl⁻ into the lumen when the stomach is empty
  • This prompts paracellular transport of Na⁺ from the bloodstream into the lumen to balance the electrochemical gradient
  • This creates an osmotic gradient, drawing water from the bloodstream into the lumen
  • Generally occurs when the stomach is empty

Water reabsorption into the bloodstream

  • After eating, the GI lumen has lots of glucose, Na⁺ and amino acids
  • These are transported from the lumen to the capillaries using apical and basolateral transporters
  • Na⁺ is transported transcellularly and creates an osmotic gradient which water follows
  • Generally occurs when the stomach is full, after a meal

How ion channels are affected in secretory diarrhoea

  • Increased activation of the apical Cl⁻ channels → increased Cl⁻ into the lumen
  • → increased paracellular Na⁺ transport from blood to lumen → increased water secretion into the lumen
  • In inflammatory diarrhoea, reduced absorption predominates

How maldigestion leads to osmotic diarrhoea

  • Important food constituents (glucose, Na⁺, amino acids) normally transported to the blood to aid water reabsorption are not present, because the body has not broken down the food
  • Secreted water therefore remains in the GI tract → osmotic diarrhoea (sorbitol also retains water in the GI tract)

6 Treatment Challenges

Why each type is difficult to treat

  • Inflammatory: anti-inflammatory drugs are designed for colon absorption, so only work if the affected area is the colon; they also have significant side effects
  • Secretory: drugs targeting transporters (e.g. SGLT-2 inhibitors) could have adverse side effects because the transporters are present elsewhere (heart, kidneys)
  • Osmotic (maldigestion): exogenous enzymes are susceptible to digestion so may be ineffective; it's difficult to enhance endogenous enzyme activity; gene therapy risks unwanted immune responses

SGLT-1 vs SGLT-2

  • SGLT-1: primarily reabsorbs glucose in the small intestine
  • SGLT-2: primarily reabsorbs glucose in the kidney

7 IBS vs IBD

Difference between IBS and IBD

  • There is a lot of overlap, but IBD is associated more with blood in stool and feverish symptoms (due to inflammation), which is not present in IBS
  • IBD is considered to be more serious

Symptom comparison

Unique to IBSUnique to IBDCommon to both
Alternating constipation and diarrhoea; bloating Weight loss; fever; blood in stool Faecal urgency; abdominal pain; mucus in stool; fatigue

Definition Abdominal tenderness means pain/discomfort when pressure is applied to a specific area of the abdomen — typically indicating soreness or inflammation in the abdominal organs.

Steps in the assessment of diarrhoea

  • Determine onset, duration, frequency and severity of symptoms
  • Enquire about red flag symptoms
  • Attempt to ascertain the underlying cause
  • Assess for complications of diarrhoea
  • Perform an abdominal examination; consider a rectal examination

Management of Crohn's disease

  • Conservative: smoking cessation, diet, avoid triggers, high energy supplements
  • Inducing remission: glucocorticoids (prednisolone, budesonide); 2nd line 5-ASA/aminosalicylate (mesalazine); infliximab/adalimumab or ustekinumab/vedolizumab if other therapies fail
  • Maintaining remission: azathioprine or mercaptopurine; 2nd line methotrexate
  • Surgical aims: conserve as much bowel as possible, alleviate complications, achieve best quality of life
  • Surgical types: bowel resection, stricturoplasty (widen a narrowed section), peri-anal fistulae, draining of perianal abscess

Crohn's disease — Post-Session summary

  • A lifelong condition where parts of the digestive system become inflamed; one type of IBD
  • Symptoms usually start in childhood or early adulthood (affects all ages)
  • Main symptoms: diarrhoea, stomach aches/cramps, blood in poo, fatigue, weight loss
  • Other symptoms: high temperature, nausea/vomiting, joint pains, sore red eyes, painful red swollen skin (usually legs), mouth ulcers
  • Symptoms may be constant or come and go (a return is a "flare-up"); children may grow more slowly
  • See a GP if: blood in poo, diarrhoea >7 days, frequent stomach aches/cramps, unexplained weight loss or slow growth
  • Diagnosis: GP asks about symptoms, diet, recent travel, medicines, family history; examines the tummy, takes blood, requests a stool sample. A gastroenterologist may do a colonoscopy, biopsy, or MRI/CT scan
  • Treatment: no cure, but treatment reduces/controls symptoms — medicines to reduce inflammation (usually steroid tablets) and medicines to stop inflammation coming back
