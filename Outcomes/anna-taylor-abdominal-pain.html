Key definitions — upper abdomen

  • Cholangitis: swelling (inflammation) of the bile duct system resulting from infection; the infection usually comes from the duodenum and ascends the biliary tree (hence "ascending cholangitis")
  • Perforated ulcer: a stomach ulcer that has eroded completely through the stomach wall, allowing stomach contents to leak into the abdominal cavity
  • Abscess: an enclosed collection of pus in tissues, organs, or confined spaces; a sign of infection, usually swollen and inflamed

Splenomegaly and spleen rupture

  • Most common cause of splenomegaly: mononucleosis (a contagious viral infection causing fatigue, fever, sore throat; spread through saliva)
  • Other causes: infections (bacterial/viral/parasitic), liver disease, blood disease, cancers, metabolic disorders, autoimmune conditions
  • Spleen rupture: a medical emergency where the spleen bursts, causing internal bleeding. Causes: forceful blow to the upper left abdomen/left lower chest, car/sporting accidents, fractured rib (spleen sits between ribs 9–11), or an enlarged spleen

Umbilical region: appendicitis, Meckel's, lymphoma

  • Appendicitis: when the appendix becomes inflamed and swollen
  • Mesenteric lymphadenitis: swelling of the lymph nodes in the abdomen
  • Meckel's diverticulum: a common congenital abnormality of the small intestine from incomplete obliteration of the vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct — a small protrusion on the small intestine (congenital, unlike acquired diverticulosis). Meckel's diverticulitis is its inflammation
  • Lymphoma: a cancer derived from lymphocytes (found anywhere in the bloodstream, not the bone marrow)

2 Urogenital & Pelvic Causes

Ureteric colic and pyelonephritis

  • Ureteric colic: abdominal pain commonly caused by obstruction of the ureter from dislodged kidney stones; can radiate to the loins and lower back
  • Pyelonephritis: a kidney infection, usually when bacteria from the bladder spread to the kidneys (also known as acute kidney infection)
  • Bacteria reach the kidneys via vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) — urine flowing backward from the bladder into the ureters (and kidneys), carrying bacteria; inadequate bladder emptying may cause this reflux

Testicular torsion

  • Occurs when the spermatic cord twists, cutting off blood supply to the testicle
  • The testes lose oxygen, nutrient supply and warm temperature, so spermatogenesis and testosterone secretion are compromised

Gynaecological/obstetric causes

  • Ovarian cysts: fluid-filled sacs on an ovary; caused by hormonal problems, pregnancy, pelvic infections, endometriosis, certain medications
  • Cystitis: inflammation of the bladder
  • Placenta: a temporary organ connecting a foetus to the uterine wall during pregnancy
  • Placental abruption: when the placenta separates early from the uterus before childbirth, disrupting nutrient/oxygen exchange → foetal distress or stillbirth

Left iliac inflammatory causes

  • Ulcerative colitis: a chronic IBD causing ulcers in the large intestine and rectum; recurring diarrhoea and stomach pain
  • Constipation: a condition making bowel movements difficult, or causing hard, dry stools

3 Gallstones

What are gallstones (cholelithiasis)?

  • Hardened deposits of bile that form in the gallbladder
  • Form when there's an imbalance in the chemical composition of bile (excess cholesterol, bile salts, or bilirubin)

Why can gallstones be difficult to identify clinically?

  • ~10–25% of the population have gallstones
  • Only ~10% of these people experience pain
  • Usually larger gallstones cause problems in the biliary tree; smaller stones can be excreted through faeces

Bile release and the mechanism of gallstone pain

  • Enteroendocrine cells (duodenal I cells) in the small intestine detect high fat content after a meal
  • This induces secretion of cholecystokinin (CCK) from the duodenum → gallbladder contraction and bile secretion into the biliary tree
  • Contraction against stones is painful — producing colicky pain (in waves, intermittent)

Clinical signs of cholelithiasis; ultrasound appearance

  • Severe epigastric/right hypochondriac pain, radiation of pain to the scapula, exacerbation after eating
  • Gallstones are hyperechoic — they appear very white/bright on ultrasound because they are denser than surrounding structures and reflect more sound waves

4 How Gallstones Form

Composition of bile

  • Water (97–98%), bile salts (0.7%), fats (0.5%), bilirubin (0.2%)

The three factors increasing stone formation

Supersaturation→ Nucleation→ Hypomotility
  • Supersaturation: too much cholesterol or bilirubin. Cholesterol is stored in vesicles in the gallbladder; excess cholesterol combines vesicles → cholesterol crystal formation
  • Nucleation: many cholesterol crystals accumulate into a tight "nucleus"; precipitated by Neutrophil Extracellular Traps (NETs), which accelerate gallstone formation
  • Hypomotility

Neutrophil Extracellular Traps (NETs) and stone growth

  • A NET is a web-like structure of DNA and proteins that neutrophils release to fight infections
  • Formed in response to infection, cholesterol crystals, or bile salt precipitation
  • Primary function: to trap and kill pathogens during immune responses
  • In gallstones: by releasing DNA/nucleic acids, NETs coat cholesterol crystals with material recognisable for clearance; their sticky, dense nature encourages material aggregation and stone growth (nucleation)

Overall mechanism and most common stone type

  • Increased cholesterol → cholesterol crystal formation (exacerbated by NETs) → increased crystal size → gallstone formation
  • Most common: cholesterol gallstones (hardened cholesterol, usually yellow-green)

Risk factors for gallstone formation

  • Obesity, haemolytic anaemia (increased bilirubin from RBC breakdown), hyperlipidaemia, Crohn's disease, female sex
  • Crohn's: affects the terminal ileum (responsible for bile acid absorption); bile acid malabsorption alters bile composition, oversaturating bile with cholesterol
  • Female sex: higher sex hormone levels (pregnancy, contraceptive pill). As cholesterol is the precursor for all sex steroids, higher steroid breakdown increases serum cholesterol → gallstone formation

5 Biliary Disease

Cholecystitis

  • Inflammation of the gallbladder, arising from gallstones damaging the internal mucosal layer (accounts for ~90% of cases)
  • Gallstones are usually harmless unless contracted against/obstructive (causing colicky pain); prolonged damage/inflammation (acute cholecystitis) can turn pain from colicky → constant

Clinical signs of cholecystitis; Murphy's sign

  • Severe epigastric/right hypochondriac pain, radiation to scapula, fever/nausea/vomiting, Murphy sign, emphysematous cholecystitis
  • Murphy's sign: a physical exam to help diagnose acute cholecystitis — if pain occurs on inspiration when the inflamed gallbladder contacts the examiner's hand, it is positive
  • Emphysematous cholecystitis: a rare, potentially fatal infection involving gas in the gallbladder wall or lumen (from gas-producing bacteria)

Ascending cholangitis — pathophysiology

  • Inflammation of the bile duct, usually caused by a bacterial infection of the biliary tree
  • Infection + obstruction cause back-pressure, allowing bacteria to spread through the tree
  • Leads to systemic inflammation (fever) and further narrowing of the bile duct → obstruction
  • Reduces clearance of bilirubin → jaundice (icterus), a characteristic sign

Charcot's triad and Reynolds' pentad

Charcot's triadReynolds' pentad
Abdominal pain, jaundice, fever Charcot's triad + confusion + hypotension

Note Confusion and hypotension indicate systemic damage spread wider than the biliary tree (concern for sepsis). Effects of cholangitis also include hepatomegaly and icterus.

6 Complications & Surgery

Gallstone ileus

  • When a gallstone blocks the small intestine (ileum)
  • Usually affects the ileocaecal valve — the narrowest region of the intestine
  • Caused by passing of a gallstone from the bile ducts into the intestinal lumen through a fistula

Complications from gallstone obstruction of the biliary tree

  • Gallstone ileus, gallstone pancreatitis, liver cirrhosis, hepatic abscess, gallbladder wall ischaemia

Counselling before laparoscopic cholecystectomy

  • Knowledge of procedure, explanation of diagnosis, treatment options, purpose of procedure, risks

7 Gallbladder & Liver Anatomy

Post-Session — structure, function, and the flow of blood and bile.

The gallbladder

  • A small, hollow organ about the size and shape of a pear; part of the biliary system
  • Lies in the right upper quadrant, affixed to the undersurface of the liver at the gallbladder fossa
  • Connected to the rest of the biliary tree by the cystic duct
  • Function: store and concentrate bile, released into the duodenum during digestion
  • Most common treatment for gallbladder pathologies: cholecystectomy

Bile — formation and function

  • Formed in the liver; an alkaline fluid continuously produced by the liver
  • Essential for digesting fats, excreting cholesterol, and has antimicrobial activity (helps digest/absorb lipids that aren't water-soluble)
  • CCK is released by duodenal I cells

The hepatic lobule

  • A hexagonally-shaped functional unit of the liver, made of liver cells, blood vessels, and bile ducts
  • Portal triads sit at each corner; a central vein sits in the middle. Each portal triad is surrounded by three lobules
  • Keeps structures compact and adjacent so hepatocytes can perform their metabolic duties well
  • Portal triad branches: hepatic artery branch, portal vein branch, bile duct

Blood flow through the lobule; sinusoids

  • Blood enters via the hepatic artery and portal vein → passes through the sinusoids → exits via the central vein → hepatic vein → inferior vena cava → heart
  • Hepatic sinusoids: where portal venous blood and hepatic arterial blood join to supply hepatocytes; hepatocytes have a sinusoid-facing side and a bile-canaliculi-facing side
  • Hepatic artery: brings O₂-rich blood to support hepatocyte energy demands
  • Portal vein: handles mixed venous blood from the GI tract so hepatocytes can process nutrients, detoxify blood, and excrete waste
  • Epithelial-lined (fenestrated) sinusoids allow easy exchange of nutrients, oxygen, and waste between blood and hepatocytes

The bile drainage pathway

Hepatocytes→ Bile canaliculi→ Canals of Hering→ Bile ductules→ Interlobular bile ducts→ L & R hepatic ducts→ Common hepatic duct→ Common bile duct (+ cystic duct)→ Duodenum via ampulla of Vater
  • Bile canaliculi: specialised channels connected to hepatocytes that drain bile
  • Canals of Hering: small openings connecting bile canaliculi to interlobular bile ducts (indirectly), transporting bile onward

Gallbladder wall structure and development

  • Innermost mucosal layer: columnar epithelium with microvilli (increase surface area for concentrating bile). Beneath: lamina propria, a smooth muscle layer, and an outer serosal layer
  • Development: the gallbladder and biliary system develop from the foregut. By the end of the 4th week, the hepatic diverticulum appears, becoming the liver, extrahepatic biliary system, and part of the pancreas; the superior bud develops into the gallbladder
  • Cholecysto-enteric fistula: an abnormal passageway between the gallbladder and GI tract → can cause small bowel obstruction
