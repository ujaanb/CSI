1 Crohn's Disease Review

Pre-Reading — recap of Crohn's disease, its presentation and diagnosis.

What is Crohn's disease?

  • A long-term condition that causes inflammation of the lining of the digestive system
  • Usually diagnosed between the ages of 15 and 40
  • Symptoms depend on which part of the digestive system is inflamed

Symptoms of Crohn's disease

  • Common: recurring diarrhoea, abdominal pain/cramping (usually worse after eating), extreme tiredness, unintended weight loss, blood and mucus in poo
  • Less common: fever ≥38°C, nausea, vomiting, joint pain and swelling (arthritis), uveitis, painful red swollen skin (most often the legs), mouth ulcers, anal pain and discharge (perianal Crohn's)
  • Children may grow at a slower rate than expected

Remission vs flare-up

  • Remission: long periods without symptoms, or very mild symptoms
  • Flare-up/relapse: periods where the symptoms are particularly troublesome

Cause and risk factors

  • The exact cause is unknown — a combination of factors may be responsible
  • Genetics (inherited genes)
  • Immune system problem (attacks healthy gut bacteria, causing inflammation)
  • Environmental factors (air pollution, medication, previous infections)
  • Smoking — smokers are twice as likely to develop Crohn's, and usually have more severe symptoms

Diagnosis — GP and gastroenterologist

  • GP questions: pattern of symptoms, diet, recent travel, medication, family history of bowel conditions
  • GP tests: pulse, blood pressure, height/weight, temperature, examine the abdomen, blood tests (inflammation, infection, anaemia), stool sample (qFIT for blood/mucus), faecal calprotectin (infection/inflammation)
  • Gastroenterologist tests: gastroscopy, colonoscopy, sigmoidoscopy, colon capsule endoscopy (pill camera), barium enema X-ray, MRI or CT scan

2 Treatment of Crohn's Disease

Aim and lines of treatment

  • No cure, but treatment can improve symptoms and keep inflammation under control
  • First-line: steroids/corticosteroids (prednisolone or budesonide) to reduce inflammation quickly
  • Follow-up: immunosuppressants and biological therapies
  • Immunosuppressants: azathioprine, mercaptopurine, methotrexate
  • Biological therapies: block particular chemicals involved in the immune response (e.g. cytokines) to reduce inflammation. Side effects: itchy skin, high temperature, joint/muscle pain, swelling of hands/lips, problems swallowing

Surgery and resection

  • An option if you choose it over medication side effects, medications don't control symptoms, quality of life is severely affected, or you have serious complications
  • Resection: removing the inflamed area of the intestine
  • After resection: healthy sections may be joined back together, OR the end of the small bowel is joined to the skin of your tummy (an ileostomy/stoma) — poo collects in special bags

Lifestyle, diet, and fertility

  • Dietary: eat 5–6 small meals rather than 3 main meals; eat a healthy, varied diet (fruit, vegetables, nuts, seeds, proteins, wholegrains); drink plenty of water
  • Lifestyle: stop smoking, especially if surgery is needed
  • Fertility: a woman's chance of becoming pregnant isn't usually affected, but surgery might affect fertility; avoid getting pregnant during a flare-up until it's controlled

Complications of Crohn's

  • Over time, inflammation can damage the digestive system, causing: narrowing of the bowel (stricture) and a channel between the bowel and skin/another section of bowel/an organ (fistula)
  • These usually need surgical treatment

3 SOCRATES & The Acute Abdomen

Main Session — characterising pain and comparing acute abdominal presentations.

How do we gather information about a patient's pain?

SOCRATES: Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associated symptoms, Timing, Exacerbating factors, Severity.

Comparison of acute abdominal presentations

SiteCharacterExacerbated by
AppendicitisStarts umbilical, then RLQConsistent with intermittent crampsMovement, coughing
Small bowel obstructionUmbilicalColicky and severeOral intake (eating/drinking)
PancreatitisEpigastric/LUQ, radiating to the backConstant and severeMovement

All four are described as severe.

Associated symptoms and signs

  • Appendicitis: anorexia (very common), nausea/vomiting, failure to pass flatus or stool, Rovsing's sign
  • Small bowel obstruction: anorexia, nausea/vomiting, failure to pass flatus or stool, abdominal distension/bloating
  • Pancreatitis: nausea/vomiting, anorexia, dyspnoea
  • Cholecystitis: nausea/vomiting, Murphy's sign

Key signs

  • Rovsing's sign: right lower abdominal pain upon palpation of the left side of the lower abdomen (positive)
  • Murphy's sign: pain on inspiration when the inflamed gallbladder contacts the examiner's hand (positive) — used to help diagnose acute cholecystitis

4 Bowel Obstruction

Symptoms and severity

  • Small bowel obstruction symptoms: nausea and vomiting, abdominal pain, bowels not opening, not passing wind, bloated and burping
  • Large bowel obstruction is more severe than small bowel obstruction

Definition The common H antigen is a sugar molecule found on virtually all red blood cells that acts as a precursor to the A and B antigens.

Haemolytic disease of the foetus and newborn (HDFN)

  • Antibodies from the mother cross the placenta and recognise the foetal RBCs as foreign → haemolysis in the fetus
  • Only occurs when the mother has a different blood group to the foetus

Why not give RhD positive blood to a woman wanting a baby in future?

  • For a woman with RhD negative blood, exposure to the RhD antigen could be problematic for future pregnancy
  • Exposure elicits an immune response, producing anti-RhD antibodies
  • If a future fetus is RhD positive, these antibodies could cross the placenta → fetal haemolysis (HDFN)

6 Losing the Large Intestine

Physiological consequences and adaptations

  • Consequences: water and electrolyte loss (e.g. Na⁺)
  • Adaptations: muscle hypertrophy and hyperplasia, aldosterone up-regulation, hypomotility (slower gut transit gives more time for absorption)

Ileostomy vs colostomy appearance

ColostomyIleostomy
Flatter and more flushed with the skin (large bowel content is more solid) More bumpy and sticks out a lot more (small bowel content is more liquid)

7 Strictures

Post-Session — stricture formation and surgical repair.

What is a stricture, and its significance?

  • Chronic inflammation in the intestines can cause the walls to thicken or form scar tissue, narrowing a section of intestine (a stricture)
  • Symptoms: nausea, vomiting, severe cramping, constipation
  • Important to repair, because narrowing could cause a blockage preventing stool from passing through

Strictureplasty

  • A surgical procedure to repair a stricture by widening the narrowed area without removing any intestine
  • Initial treatment for inflammatory strictures is typically medication to improve the narrowing
  • Surgical options: strictureplasty and small bowel resection
  • Most effective in the ileum and jejunum; less effective in the duodenum
  • How it's performed: the surgeon cuts lengthwise along the narrowed area, then sews the intestine crosswise — shortening and widening the affected part so food can pass through

Why is strictureplasty preferred over resection?

  • Removing portions of the small intestine can cause complications such as short bowel syndrome
  • Short bowel syndrome: occurs when large sections of small intestine are removed and the body is unable to absorb adequate nutrients and water

8 Fistulising Crohn's Disease

What is a fistula, and fistulising Crohn's?

  • Fistula: an abnormal connection or tunnelling between the intestines and a nearby organ or skin
  • Fistulising Crohn's disease: a complication where a chronic tract forms between two epithelial surfaces
  • Fistulas form when inflammation causes sores or ulcers on the inside wall of the intestine or nearby organs

Types of fistula and where they connect

FistulaConnection
PerianalAnal canal/rectum ↔ surface of skin in the anal area
Rectovaginal / ColovaginalIntestines ↔ vagina
EnteroentericOne part of the intestines ↔ another part of the intestines
Enterocutaneous / ColocutaneousIntestines ↔ surface of the skin
Enterovesical / ColovesicalIntestines ↔ bladder

Symptoms by fistula type

  • Perianal: tender swelling/lump around the anus; pain and irritation when sitting/moving/coughing or during a bowel movement; drainage/discharge; bleeding
  • Rectovaginal/colovaginal: passing air; stool or pus through the vagina; brown/foul-smelling discharge; painful intercourse. Treatment (rectovaginal): vaginal advancement flap, LIFT procedure
  • Enteroenteric: diarrhoea (can feel like a flare), poor nutrient absorption, dehydration; possibly no symptoms at all
  • Enterocutaneous/colocutaneous: leaking of stool (may smell foul), brown discharge, redness, pain
  • Enterovesical: passing air/stool/pus when urinating, foul-smelling urine, urine leaking from anus, frequent urination
