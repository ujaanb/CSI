1 Depression Basics

Pre-Session — what depression is and how it is managed.

What is depression, and how does it differ from low mood?

  • Depression is more than simply feeling unhappy or fed up for a few days.
  • When depressed, you feel persistently sad for weeks or months, rather than just a few days.
  • A low mood may improve after a short period of time, rather than being a sign of depression.

Main symptom and physical symptoms of depression

  • Main symptom associated: anxiety
  • Physical symptoms: feeling constantly tired, sleeping badly, having no appetite or sex drive, various aches and pains

Describe an exacerbating cause of depression.

  • Sometimes there's a trigger — life-changing events such as bereavement, losing your job, or giving birth can bring it on
  • People with a family history of depression are more likely to experience it
  • You can also become depressed for no obvious reason

Lifestyle and social changes to treat/improve depression

  • Lifestyle: exercise, guided self-help, cutting down on alcohol, giving up smoking, eating healthily
  • Social: talking therapies, reading a self-help book, joining a support group

2 Classification: ICD & DSM

Main Session — how diseases and mental health disorders are classified and diagnosed.

What is the ICD?

  • The International Classification of Diseases — a comprehensive system for classifying diseases and health conditions, including physical illnesses, injuries, and mental health disorders
  • Latest version: ICD-11

What is the DSM?

  • The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders — provides clear, specific criteria for diagnosing mental health disorders, ensuring consistency across practitioners and settings
  • Latest version: DSM-5 (DSM-V)
  • Depression is diagnosed in the UK using the DSM criteria

How is the DSM used to diagnose depression?

At least one key symptom and five total symptoms are required.

The two key symptoms (DSM)

  • Persistent sadness or low mood
  • Marked loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia)

Total symptoms (DSM)

  • Disturbed sleep
  • Decreased or increased appetite and/or weight
  • Fatigue or loss of energy (anergia)
  • Agitation or slowing of movements
  • Poor concentration or indecisiveness
  • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive/inappropriate guilt
  • Suicidal thoughts or acts

3 The Biopsychosocial Model

Demographics of self-harm and suicide

  • Self-harm most common: females aged 17–19
  • Suicide most common: males aged 40–59

Factors that may contribute to depression (biopsychosocial)

4 Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)

What is an ACE and the ACE score?

  • An Adverse Childhood Experience (ACE) is a traumatic or stressful event occurring during childhood (before age 18) that can significantly impact mental, emotional, and physical well-being later in life
  • The ACE score is a numerical measure of a person's exposure to ACEs

How the ACE score is determined — the 10 ACEs

The original ACE questionnaire has 10 questions grouped into three categories; each "yes" adds 1 point.

AbuseNeglectHousehold Dysfunction
Physical abuse, emotional abuse, sexual abuse Physical neglect, emotional neglect Substance abuse in the household, mental illness in the household, domestic violence, parental separation/divorce, incarceration of a household member
  • 0 ACEs: no exposure to the 10 categories
  • 1–3 ACEs: low to moderate risk of health/social issues, depending on protective factors
  • 4 or more ACEs: high risk of negative health outcomes (chronic illness, mental health disorders, risky behaviours)

5 Statistics for Studies

What is a p-value?

  • The probability of an event happening if the null hypothesis were true (the probability of a desired outcome occurring by chance)
  • Example: a p-value of 0.03 means a 3% probability that the observed effect (or more extreme) would occur if the null hypothesis were true. As this is below the typical 0.05 threshold, the null hypothesis is rejected and the drug is considered effective.

What is bias in a study?

Any systematic error or influence that distorts the findings or conclusions of a study.

Statistical vs clinical significance

  • Statistical significance: whether the observed effect is unlikely to have occurred by chance, based on a predefined threshold (usually a p-value of 0.05 or lower)
  • Clinical significance: whether the effect is meaningful/relevant in a real-world clinical context — whether it has practical importance for patients or practitioners
  • A result can be statistically significant but not clinically significant, and vice versa

Impact of drugs and placebos on depression

As the severity of depression increases, drugs appear to have more of a positive effect on patients with depression.

6 Serotonin Pharmacology

Mechanisms of antidepressants acting on the serotonin system.

Mechanism of SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors)

  • Work by stopping serotonin from being reused, by binding to the serotonin transporter (SERT) and blocking serotonin transport
  • SERT is responsible for terminating serotonin signalling
  • When blocked, serotonin accumulates in the synaptic space, effectively keeping the serotonin signal "on", helping alleviate depression symptoms

Post-synaptic serotonin receptor agonists

Mimic the effects of serotonin by stimulating the serotonin receptor (affecting happiness, sexual function, pain perception, cardiovascular function, social behaviour and impulsivity).

Tryptophan hydroxylase (TPH) and its inhibitors

  • TPH is the enzyme used to produce 5-HTP, a precursor of serotonin
  • TPH inhibitors reduce the production of serotonin, which reduces its prevalence in the synaptic space

Monoamine oxidase (MAO) and MAO inhibitors

  • MAO is an enzyme critical in the breakdown of neurotransmitters, including serotonin, in the brain and body
  • MAO-A primarily breaks down serotonin, norepinephrine and dopamine; MAO-B primarily breaks down dopamine and related compounds
  • Physiologically, MAO breaks down serotonin in the presynaptic cleft, reducing its concentration and allowing serotonin to flow back into the presynaptic cleft
  • MAO inhibitors disrupt the concentration gradient of serotonin across the presynaptic membrane; by increasing presynaptic serotonin levels, they increase synaptic serotonin and give antidepressant-like effects

Serotonin auto-receptors and their agonists

  • A serotonin autoreceptor is found on presynaptic neurons that release serotonin; these receptors regulate the release and synthesis of serotonin
  • Auto-receptor agonists work by stimulating the auto-receptors, leading to the release and synthesis of serotonin in the brain

7 Counselling & Safety

Three things to tell patients before starting antidepressants

  • Drugs take several weeks to work
  • Symptoms may worsen initially
  • Need to continue for ~six months after remission of symptoms
  • Need to wean the drugs gradually — sudden stoppage may lead to unwanted side effects
  • Antidepressants interact with, and are contraindicated with, many drugs — patients should inform us of all current medications

What is serotonin syndrome?

  • A potentially life-threatening condition caused by excessive levels of serotonin in the brain
  • Typically occurs from medication interactions, overdose, or excessive use of drugs that increase serotonin activity
  • Symptoms: agitation, muscle rigidity, fever, and autonomic dysfunction
