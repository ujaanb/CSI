2 The Dorsal Column–Medial Lemniscus Pathway

Structure of dorsal column neurons from limbs to brain

  • Primary neurones: lower/upper limb → caudal medulla. Pass through the gracile/cuneate tracts and synapse at the gracile/cuneate nuclei. All primary neurones pass through the dorsal root ganglia.
  • Secondary neurones: gracile/cuneate nucleus of the caudal medulla → thalamus. The gracile and cuneate tracts join in a connected tract called the medial lemniscus. Secondary neurones cross (decussate) at the caudal medulla to the opposite side and synapse in the thalamus.
  • Tertiary neurones: thalamus → somatosensory cortex, where sensory information is processed.

3 The Spinothalamic Tract

Structure of spinothalamic neurons from limbs to brain

  • Primary neurones: lower/upper limb → spinal cord. Instead of synapsing at the medulla (like the dorsal column pathway), they synapse at the spinal cord level of entry.
  • Secondary neurones: spinal cord → thalamus. These decussate at the lower part of the spinal cord (where they initially synapsed) to reach the opposite side of the brain.
  • Tertiary neurones: thalamus → somatosensory cortex, where sensory information is processed.

Course of right-sided pain from receptor to brain

  • Pain is received by nociceptors at the point of contact
  • Relayed via the primary neurone from nociceptor → spinal cord
  • The secondary neurone decussates within the spinal cord to the left side before synapsing at the thalamus (spinal cord → thalamus)
  • The tertiary neurone goes thalamus → somatosensory cortex

4 Pain Fibres & the Reticular Formation

A-delta vs C fibres

A-delta fibresC fibres
Faster, myelinated; responsible for sharp pain, touch, and motor control Slower, unmyelinated; carry dull pain, temperature, and autonomic signals

Course of A-delta and C fibres within the spinothalamic tract

  • A-delta: receptor → dorsal horn → thalamus → somatosensory cortex → SII region → insula
  • C fibres: receptor → dorsal horn → reticular formation → thalamus → somatosensory cortex → cingulate cortex → amygdala

The reticular formation

  • A complex network of neurons that process specific types of sensory information before they reach the brain
  • Course: neurons running from the pons to the midbrain
  • Function: enhances perception and alertness to a specific stimulus — a way of arousing yourself to become more aware
  • Which fibres run through it: C fibres (carrying dull pain)
  • Note Sharp pain does not go through the reticular formation because it takes the quickest route to the brain — the reticular formation would slow transmission, which is inappropriate when a quick response to pain is needed

6 Referred Pain

The science of referred pain

  • The body is generally bad at interpreting where visceral pain is coming from
  • This is because the primary neurones of both visceral and somatic sensory neurones synapse within the spinal cord
  • For this reason, pain can be referred and interpreted as coming from another region of the skin

Where liver/gallbladder pain refers to

  • The right shoulder area
  • Due to potential compression of the diaphragm and/or phrenic nerve, which leads to pain being referred there

7 Radiculopathy & Cauda Equina Syndrome

What is radiculopathy?

Commonly referred to as a pinched nerve — injury or damage to nerve roots in the area where they leave the spine.

What is cauda equina syndrome (CES)?

A condition that occurs when the nerves at the bottom of the spinal cord are compressed. Most common cause: vertebral disc herniation.

Distinguishing CES from radiculopathy

RadiculopathyCauda equina syndrome
Usually unilateral — nerve root compressed on one side; presents as motor and/or sensory dysfunction on one side Usually presents with bilateral motor or sensory dysfunction, because the main body of the spinal cord is affected
