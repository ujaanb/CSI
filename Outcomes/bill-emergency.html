1 GCS & the ABCDE Approach

Pre-Session — the core tools for assessing a critically ill patient.

What is GCS and what is it used for?

  • The Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) is a widely used tool to assess a person's level of consciousness, particularly after a head injury or in cases of altered mental status.

What is the GCS scored out of, and what are its three sections?

  • Scored out of 15.
  • Eye Opening Response
  • Verbal Response
  • Motor Response

What is meant by the ABCDE approach?

  • A structured, systematic method used in emergency medical settings to assess and manage critically ill or injured patients.
  • A mnemonic covering various systems of an individual to assess the severity of their illness.

What does each letter stand for in ABCDE?

Airway→ Breathing→ Circulation→ Disability→ Exposure

When is the ABCDE assessment most likely to be useful?

In medical emergencies, to assess and manage critically ill or injured patients.

2 Airway

What are we looking for during Airway?

  • Is the patient's airway secure or compromised? (i.e. safe and patent)

When can we give the "green light" to Airway?

The airway can be considered safe if the patient is able to speak.

Describe two problems that may be observed during Airway assessment.

  • Paradoxical chest and abdominal movements
  • Use of accessory muscles
  • Partial obstruction → noisy breathing (snoring, stridor, wheeze) with diminished air entry
  • Total obstruction → no breath sounds at the nose or mouth
  • Central cyanosis is a late sign

What is cyanosis?

  • A change of body tissue colour to a bluish-purple hue, resulting from a decrease in the amount of oxygen bound to haemoglobin in the red blood cells of the capillary bed.
  • It results from decreased oxygen circulating around the body.
  • Note Haemoglobin is bright red in the oxygenated state, but purplish-blue in the reduced state.

Describe three possible resolutions for Airway problems.

  • Airway opening manoeuvres (head tilt and chin lift, or jaw thrust)
  • Suction to remove debris (never use your fingers!)
  • Simple airway adjuncts (nasopharyngeal airway, oropharyngeal/Guedel airway)
  • Supraglottic airway (i.e. iGel)
  • Advanced airway interventions — intubation, emergency surgical airway
  • THEN give oxygen at high concentration

What is an important consideration for Airway problems?

If GCS < 8, then intubate.

What is a simple airway adjunct? What is intubation?

  • Airway adjuncts are devices used to help secure an open airway or manage airway complications.
  • Intubation (tracheal intubation) is a medical procedure that involves inserting a flexible plastic tube into the trachea to keep the airway open.

3 Breathing

What are we looking for during Breathing?

  • Obtain oxygen saturations (pulse oximeter) and count respiratory rate (RR)
  • Can they talk in sentences? Words? Not at all?
  • Look for use of respiratory muscles, central cyanosis, sweating
  • Look/feel for symmetry of chest movement and for chest deformity
  • Feel for the trachea — deviation may indicate tension pneumothorax or large effusion on the contralateral side, or collapse on the ipsilateral side
  • Percuss the chest — hyper-resonance may suggest a pneumothorax; dullness usually indicates consolidation or pleural fluid
  • Listen to the chest — for air entry and added sounds (crackles, wheeze, stridor). Absent/reduced sounds suggest pneumothorax, pleural fluid, or consolidation

Describe problems that may be observed during Breathing assessment.

  • Reduced GCS
  • Acute severe asthma or COPD
  • Pneumonia or lung infection
  • Pulmonary oedema
  • Pneumothorax / tension pneumothorax
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Haemothorax
  • Respiratory depression
  • Tracheal deviation
  • Asymmetry of chest movement
  • Chest deformity
  • Central cyanosis / sweating

Describe three possible resolutions for Breathing problems.

  • All critically ill patients should be given oxygen — target saturation >94% for most patients
  • If rate/depth of breathing is insufficient or absent, use bag-mask or pocket mask ventilation; if no improvement, non-invasive ventilation or intubation may be required
  • Intramuscular adrenaline in anaphylaxis
  • Chest drainage in pneumothorax
  • Naloxone in opioid overdose
  • Bronchodilators in airway disease

What is Naloxone?

A medicine that rapidly reverses an opioid overdose — it attaches to opioid receptors and reverses/blocks the effects of other opioids.

Quick facts

  • Normal respiratory rate: 12–20 breaths/min
  • Maximum oxygen that can be given: 15 L
  • Stridor: an abnormal, high-pitched respiratory sound produced by irregular airflow in a narrowed airway
  • Patency: the state of the airways being open or unobstructed

4 Circulation

What is done during Circulation?

  • Look at the colour of the hands and digits (blue, pink, pale or mottled?)
  • Assess limb temperature by feeling the hands (cool or warm?)
  • Measure the capillary refill time (CRT)
  • Take the heart rate
  • Look at the neck for the height of the jugular venous pressure (JVP) — an elevated JVP may indicate heart failure/fluid overload
  • Apply 3-lead cardiac monitoring; also ask for a 12-lead ECG
  • Measure blood pressure (even in shock, BP may be normal due to compensatory increases in peripheral resistance)
  • Auscultate the heart — murmur? muffled sounds (cardiac tamponade)?
  • Look thoroughly for evidence of bleeding

What is capillary refill time (CRT) and how is it assessed?

  • A quick test that measures how well blood circulates in your extremities.
  • Apply cutaneous pressure for 5 seconds on a fingertip held at heart level to cause blanching (paling).
  • Measure how long it takes for skin to return to its previous colour.
  • Normal CRT is < 2 s; a prolonged CRT suggests poor peripheral perfusion (but can also be due to cold surroundings and old age).

Describe problems that may be observed during Circulation.

  • Hypovolaemia (bleeding, burns, diarrhoea/vomiting, dehydration)
  • Pump failure — cardiogenic (heart failure, MI, arrhythmia); non-cardiogenic (cardiac tamponade, tension pneumothorax, PE)
  • Vasodilation (sepsis, anaphylaxis)

Describe three possible resolutions for Circulation problems.

  • Insert one or more large IV cannula; take blood for haematology, biochemistry, coagulation, microbiology and cross-matching; a lactate level indicates tissue perfusion
  • If BP is low, give a fluid challenge — 250 ml up to 1000 ml of crystalloid (less if elderly or known heart failure); monitor HR and BP in response
  • If bleeding, replace blood with blood (rather than crystalloid)
  • If BP does not improve despite IV fluids, specific drug infusions on intensive care may improve heart function or stimulate vasoconstriction

Key definitions in Circulation

  • Cardiac tamponade: compression of the heart due to pericardial effusion
  • Hypovolaemia: a state of abnormally low extracellular fluid in the body
  • IV cannula: a small flexible plastic tube inserted into a vein to give medication/fluids directly into the bloodstream

How can burns cause hypovolaemia?

  • Skin barrier damage: burns destroy the skin's protective barrier, leading to direct loss of fluids (especially plasma)
  • Burn-induced capillary leak: inflammation increases capillary permeability, so fluids, proteins and electrolytes leak into the interstitial space (third-spacing)
  • Vasodilation from the inflammatory response decreases organ perfusion, risking organ failure

How is lactate a useful indicator of tissue perfusion?

  • Normally the body generates energy through aerobic metabolism, which uses oxygen to produce ATP; a small amount of lactate is produced and cleared by the liver/kidneys
  • When tissues are hypoxic, they switch to anaerobic glycolysis, producing lactic acid released into the bloodstream
  • Elevated lactate (hyperlactatemia, typically > 2 mmol/L) indicates compromised oxygen delivery and anaerobic metabolism

5 Disability

What is done during Disability?

Disability looks at level of consciousness and neurological functioning.

Describe problems that may be observed during Disability.

  • Profound hypoxia or hypercapnoea
  • Drugs — sedatives, opioids, toxins, poisons
  • Cerebral hypoperfusion (e.g. from profound hypotension)
  • Raised intracranial pressure
  • CVA (stroke)

Definition Hypercapnoea is a condition of abnormally elevated carbon dioxide levels in the blood.

Describe three possible resolutions for Disability problems.

  • Provide oral or parenteral glucose if needed (follow local protocols)
  • Provide analgesia for pain
  • Specific action for specific problems (treat seizures, treat opioid toxicity with Naloxone, seek specialist input if raised ICP)

6 Exposure

What is done during Exposure?

  • Full exposure of the body may be necessary to examine the patient properly
  • Respect the patient's dignity and minimise heat loss

Describe specific things done during Exposure.

  • Examine head to toe, front and back — look for bleeding, swellings, rashes, sores, wounds, catheters
  • Perform a focused exam of any relevant systems (e.g. the abdomen)
  • Take a full clinical history from the patient, relatives/friends, and other staff
  • Review the patient's notes and charts
  • Review laboratory or radiological investigations

8 Circulatory Shock

Describe the physiological response to hypotension.

  • Hypotension (low BP) reduces the baroreceptor firing rate
  • In response, the baroreceptors signal the CNS to increase heart rate to compensate
  • This is a compensatory mechanism that prevents tissue perfusion falling too low

What are the consequences of untreated hypotension?

  • The ultimate consequence is circulatory shock
  • Inadequate blood pumped around the body → reduced tissue perfusion → organ failure and death

What is circulatory shock, and what are its four types?

Circulatory shock is when inadequate blood flow results in tissue hypoperfusion.

TypeMechanism
HypovolemicSignificant loss of blood or body fluids reduces overall blood volume → tissue hypoperfusion
CardiogenicInability or failure of the heart to pump blood efficiently
ObstructivePhysical obstruction to blood flow in the heart or major vessels (PE, tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade)
DistributiveWidespread vasodilation → poor distribution of blood flow (septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic)

Causes of obstructive and distributive shock

  • Obstructive: pulmonary embolism, tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade
  • Distributive: septic shock (severe infection → systemic inflammation and vasodilation), anaphylactic shock (severe allergic reaction), neurogenic shock (spinal cord/nervous system damage → loss of vascular tone)

What is a tension pneumothorax?

When air trapped in the pleural space compresses the lungs and heart, preventing effective circulation.

Which is the most common type of circulatory shock?

Distributive, septic (62%).

Signs of tissue hypoperfusion (and syncope)

  • Syncope = fainting or passing out
  • Brain: altered mental state
  • Skin: mottled, clammy skin (bluish-red; pale and moist)
  • Kidney: oliguria (low urine output)

9 Sepsis

What are the four stages of sepsis?

SIRS→ Sepsis→ Severe Sepsis→ Septic Shock

What is SIRS?

  • Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome
  • A nonspecific inflammatory state that can be triggered by a variety of insults, not just infection
  • Describes a clinical response to inflammation that affects the entire body

What is the Sepsis Six? Recall each.

The six things that must be done in response to a patient suspected of having sepsis — remembered as "Give three, Take three":

GiveTake
O₂ to keep saturation >94%Blood cultures
IV antibioticsLactate measurement
Fluid challengeUrine output

Mnemonic "Save Sick Patients From Lethal Bugs": Send blood cultures, Start antibiotics, Provide oxygen, Fluid resuscitation, Lactate, monitor urine (Bladder).

Describe a risk of giving a fluid challenge.

  • The main risk is tissue oedema
  • Increasing blood pressure may cause leakage from capillaries
  • This can cause tissue oedema and even pulmonary oedema

Why do we measure urine output?

  • It is a marker of tissue hypoperfusion — a hypoperfused kidney produces significantly less urine
  • Urine output also measures the body's fluid balance

Sepsis: difference between sepsis and severe sepsis; and key labs

  • Sepsis: 2 of the signs of SIRS + evidence of infection + evidence of hypoperfusion or hypotension
  • Severe sepsis: the above plus failure to resolve hypotension in response to adequate fluid challenge
  • Molecule distinctly lowered in sepsis: platelets
  • GGT (gamma-glutamyl transferase): an enzyme mainly in the liver; high blood levels may indicate liver disease or bile-duct damage

10 Abdominal Anatomy

Regions, quadrants, and Post-Session organ localisation.

How many abdominopelvic regions are there? Recall them.

Nine (the perineum is often considered the tenth division):

  • Right hypochondriac
  • Epigastric
  • Left hypochondriac
  • Right lumbar
  • Umbilical
  • Left lumbar
  • Right iliac
  • Hypogastric
  • Left iliac

How many abdominopelvic quadrants are there? Recall them.

Four: Right upper (RUQ), Left upper (LUQ), Right lower (RLQ), Left lower (LLQ).

What is diverticulosis?

  • The state of the formation of diverticula within the colon
  • Diverticula are small pouches that form due to excess colonic pressure

Post-Session: organs and pain by quadrant

  • LUQ organs: medial portion of the liver, left kidney, stomach, spleen, descending colon, transverse colon, pancreas, small intestine
  • Likely significance of LUQ pain: malrotation of the intestine and colon
  • RUQ organs: liver (among others)
