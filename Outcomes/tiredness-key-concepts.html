1 Pre-Reading

Background on the everyday causes and management of tiredness.

Describe three common causes of tiredness.

  • Not getting enough sleep, or finding it hard to get enough sleep (insomnia)
  • An unhealthy lifestyle (e.g. poor diet, lack of exercise)
  • Stress, depression and dealing with life challenges (e.g. bereavement or looking after a new baby)
  • Hormonal changes (e.g. during puberty, pregnancy or menopause)
  • Illnesses (e.g. glandular fever and COVID-19)
  • Medical treatments/medicines (e.g. chemotherapy)

Recall three conditions which may cause tiredness.

  • Sleep apnoea
  • Iron deficiency anaemia
  • Diabetes
  • Overactive thyroid (hyperthyroidism)
  • Myalgic encephalomyelitis / chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS)

Recall ways to improve symptoms of tiredness/fatigue.

  • Have a healthy diet / exercise regularly
  • Sleep at the same time — aim for 6–9 hours
  • Relax a couple of hours before your sleep time
  • Create a relaxing sleeping environment (quiet, dark, warm)

Recall ways to alleviate symptoms of tiredness/fatigue.

  • Do not smoke
  • Do not drink too much alcohol
  • Do not eat, have caffeine or exercise in the hours leading to sleep
  • Do not use smartphones or screens in the hour before sleep

2 Tiredness vs Fatigue & Epidemiology

Main Session — definitions and how common fatigue is in primary care.

What is the difference between tiredness and fatigue?

  • Tiredness refers to a general symptom that almost all of us experience.
  • Fatigue refers to the long-term effects of tiredness not being resolved by sleep.

Note Roughly 7% of all GP consultations are due to fatigue.

To the nearest 10%, what percentage of patients who present to a GP with fatigue have a diagnosis made?

~66%

To the nearest 10%, what percentage of patients who present to a GP with fatigue have a condition that is detected on blood tests?

<10% — most causes are not found on blood tests, so history is key.

Recall seven conditions that could be possible causes of fatigue.

  • Anaemia
  • Sleep apnoea
  • Underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism)
  • Coeliac disease
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome
  • Diabetes
  • Glandular fever
  • Depression
  • Restless legs syndrome
  • Anxiety
  • Malignancy
  • Vitamin D deficiency
  • Heart failure
  • Infection
  • Lupus

3 Making a Differential Diagnosis

How to narrow down the cause of fatigue/tiredness.

Describe how we can make a differential diagnosis for symptoms of fatigue/tiredness.

  • Asking questions on the frequency and severity of the symptoms is often very helpful.
  • This can help us understand just how much the patient is being affected.
  • We can also gain an understanding of what conditions to rule out by asking other system-related questions and conducting tests (e.g. bloods).

4 The Thyroid HPT Axis

Why primary hypothyroidism shows a characteristic hormone pattern.

Explain why the following is observed in primary hypothyroidism: high TSH, low T3/T4.

Hypothalamus → TRH→ Anterior pituitary → TSH→ Thyroid → T3 / T4
  • Physiologically, TRH released from the hypothalamus causes TSH to be released from the anterior pituitary.
  • This causes T3 and T4 to be released from the thyroid, which both act on the anterior pituitary and hypothalamus to tell it to stop making more TRH and TSH (negative feedback).
  • In individuals with hypothyroidism, the TSH is released, however the T3 and T4 isn't released adequately.
  • This means there is a problem with the negative feedback mechanism — the T3 isn't telling the hypothalamus to stop making TRH/TSH, so these hormones are still secreted.
  • This causes high TSH levels to remain in the bloodstream and low T3/T4.

5 Hashimoto's Disease

The immune sequence that drives autoimmune thyroiditis.

Describe the sequence of events in the immune system that take place in Hashimoto's disease.

  • Physiologically in all healthy individuals, the TPO antigen is present in the thyroid gland.
    • TPO = thyroid peroxidase, the enzyme used to produce MIT and DIT in the production of T3/T4.
  • In patients with Hashimoto's, the TPO antigen is recognised as foreign by the immune system and an immune response is elicited against it.
  • The APC presents MHC II (along with the TPO antigen) on its surface, which interacts with the TCR on T cells.
    • The co-stimulatory interaction between the T cell's CD28 receptor and the B7 ligand on the APC is also integral in activating the T cell.
  • The T cell presents its CD40L on its surface, which can stimulate B cells when it interacts with the CD40 receptor on B cells.
  • B cells recognise the CD40L, and this induces the production of the anti-TPO antibody by the B cell, which goes and attacks the thyroid.
  • B cells can also act as APCs (alternative pathway).
TPO seen as foreign→ APC: MHC II + TPO → TCR→ CD28–B7 co-stim→ T cell CD40L → CD40 on B cell→ Anti-TPO antibody → thyroid

6 Immune Tolerance (Phase 1A recap)

How the body normally avoids attacking itself — and how that can fail.

What prevents immune cells from attacking host cells physiologically?

  • The presence of PAMPs helps cells to differentiate between pathogens and self cells.
  • Ultimately, there are different mechanisms within central and peripheral tolerance (such as AIRE and affinity maturation) which ensure any self-attacking cells are destroyed before being released into the circulation.

What is the difference between central and peripheral tolerance?

  • Central tolerance occurs before B and T cells have been secreted into circulation.
  • Peripheral tolerance occurs after B and T cells have been secreted into circulation.

What happens in central tolerance?

Cells that can be destructive against "self" / human antigens (and cause potential damage) are deleted and destroyed before entering into circulation.

Describe how central tolerance works in B cell selection.

  • Differentiated B cells in the bone marrow go around the bone marrow and patrol for self antigens.
  • If the bone marrow notices that a B cell receptor recognises a self antigen, then it destroys it before it is released into the bloodstream.

Describe how central tolerance works in T cell selection.

  • T cells that are unable to bind onto MHCs, or that bind to MHCs too strongly, are removed from the thymus and not released into circulation.
  • AIREs (Autoimmune Regulators) are transcription factors in the thymus, used to express the genes of peripheral tissue antigens.
  • The expression of these genes allows us to see which T cells recognise the self-antigens and destroy them accordingly.

Why is central tolerance for B cells simpler than for T cells?

  • B cells bind to antigens that are free in circulation, meaning that whether this happens in the bone marrow or not can be used to select between B cells.
  • T cells only bind to antigens in circulation on MHC molecules, so this makes things more difficult.

How can mutations in AIRE affect hosts?

  • AIRE will no longer be able to produce the self-antigens.
  • It can lead to a lack of adequate T cell selection, as the thymus no longer knows which T cells attack self-antigens.
  • This results in multi-organ autoimmunity.

How is peripheral tolerance useful?

If any T or B cells that attack self antigens slip through central tolerance by accident, they can be destroyed via peripheral tolerance.

What is affinity maturation?

  • A process where a B cell receptor can produce antibodies which bind onto a pathogen much better after repeated exposure.
  • It happens when the BCR is constantly modified to produce slightly different antibodies, and the lymph nodes select those that are best fit for the antigens.
  • It occurs in germinal centres.

What is a consequence of affinity maturation?

The modification of the antibodies can lead to certain B cells recognising self antigens and attacking self cells.

Describe how anergy works in peripheral tolerance.

  • This is when the MHC on the surface of host cells doesn't contain the necessary stimulatory molecules to activate naive T cells.
  • This means these self-attacking T cells aren't activated and are less likely to be activated in future.

Describe how ignorance works in peripheral tolerance.

  • This is where the concentration of a self antigen is too low for T cells to be destroyed (i.e. they are ignored).
  • This happens in immunologically privileged areas (e.g. CNS, eye, brain), where the risk of autoimmunity is greater than the risk of actual infection.

Describe how positive selection in B/T cell differentiation works.

  • A process used to determine whether a B/T cell can detect antigens using its BCR/TCR (its primary function).
  • If it cannot, it has developed abnormally and these cells undergo apoptosis.

Describe how negative selection in B/T cell differentiation works.

  • A process used to determine whether a B/T cell has BCRs/TCRs that react against self (host cell) antigens.
  • We don't want B/T cells that react against self antigens, so they undergo apoptosis.

Describe the role of T-regulatory cells in self-tolerance.

  • T regulatory cells (Tregs) are essential for maintaining immune homeostasis and tolerance, preventing autoimmune diseases, and regulating immune responses.
  • Their ability to suppress excessive immune activity is critical for self-tolerance, and they play significant roles in cancer, transplantation, and infectious diseases.

7 Hypothyroidism (Post-Session)

Causes, the drugs involved, and the symptom picture.

Recall five causes of hypothyroidism.

  • Autoimmune thyroid disease (e.g. Hashimoto's)
  • Radioactive iodine treatment
  • Antithyroid drugs
  • Medicines such as lithium and amiodarone
  • Cough medicines with large amounts of iodine
  • Some foods taken in excess (e.g. kelp/seaweed)
  • Pituitary malfunction
  • Radiation for head and neck cancers (uncommon in the UK)
  • Congenital hypothyroidism

What are lithium and amiodarone used for?

DrugUsed to treat
LithiumSome mental health problems
AmiodaroneSome heart problems

Recall seven symptoms of hypothyroidism.

  • Fatigue and tiredness
  • Increased awareness of the cold (cold intolerance)
  • Dry and coarse skin
  • Dry and thinning hair
  • Hoarse or croaky voice
  • Constipation
  • Muscle weakness, cramps and aches
  • Pins and needles in the fingers and hands (carpal tunnel syndrome)
  • Heavier and longer periods
  • Fertility problems
  • Low libido
  • Weight gain
  • Puffy face and bags under the eyes
  • Slow speech, movements and thoughts
  • Low mood or depression
  • Memory problems
  • Difficulty in concentration
  • Slow heart beat (bradycardia)
  • Slightly raised blood pressure (hypertension)
  • Raised cholesterol
