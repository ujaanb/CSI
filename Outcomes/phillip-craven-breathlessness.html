1 COPD

Pre-Reading — the two conditions under the COPD umbrella and their pathophysiology.

What is COPD?

  • Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease — a group of progressive lung diseases that make it difficult to breathe
  • Primarily affects the airways and lungs; often associated with long-term exposure to irritating gases or particles, such as cigarette smoke

The two conditions under the COPD umbrella

  • Emphysema — damage to the air sacs in the lungs
  • Chronic bronchitis — long-term inflammation of the airways

Note COPD is an umbrella term for emphysema and chronic bronchitis.

What is emphysema, and the role of elastin?

  • Emphysema primarily damages the alveoli, where O₂/CO₂ exchange occurs
  • Over time, the walls of the air sacs break down, merge into larger less efficient air spaces, and lose elasticity — reducing the lungs' ability to transfer oxygen and expel CO₂
  • Elastin normally pushes outwards against the forces that would otherwise cause the airways to collapse (after expiration, low airway pressure would push them inwards)

Pathophysiology of emphysema

  • Lung inflammation releases proteases and reactive oxygen species (ROS) into the lungs
  • This causes neutrophil elastase release, which breaks down elastin in the airways
  • Loss of elastin → loss of rigidity of the alveoli → they collapse
  • Main dysfunction: lack of elastic recoil in the airways/bronchi. This lowers surface area for gas exchange as parenchyma becomes dysfunctional and traps air

What is chronic bronchitis, and its pathophysiology?

  • A long-term inflammatory condition of the bronchial tubes, characterised by persistent coughing that produces mucus for at least three months in two consecutive years
  • Main issue: excess release of mucus. Irritants/chemicals from smoking cause hyperplasia and hypertrophy of bronchial mucinous glands → excess mucus secretion into the airways
  • This obstructs air getting out of the airways due to excess mucus

Aside Sputum is green due to myeloperoxidase, an iron-containing enzyme; iron turns green in mucus.

Symptoms, demographic, causes, risk factors of COPD

  • Symptoms: shortness of breath (esp. on activity), chronic cough with mucus, wheezing, chest tightness, frequent respiratory infections, fatigue
  • Demographic: mainly middle-aged or older adults who smoke; many do not realise they have it
  • Causes: long-term exposure to irritants damaging the lungs/airways; most common cause is smoking, plus environmental and genetic influences
  • Risk factors: age, smoking, environmental irritants, genetic predisposition, health history

Treatments, prognosis, and prevention

  • Treatments: stop smoking, inhalers and medicines, pulmonary rehabilitation, surgery or lung transplant, oxygen therapy
  • Prognosis: varies; cannot be cured or reversed, but treatment often keeps it controlled. In some, it worsens despite treatment, significantly impacting quality of life and leading to life-threatening problems
  • Prevention: largely preventable by avoiding smoking; stopping helps prevent further lung damage

2 Causes of Breathlessness

Main Session — dyspnoea and its differential diagnosis across systems.

What is shortness of breath?

The sensation of dyspnoea is caused by a mismatch between the brain's demand for breathing and the body's ability to meet that demand.

Conditions that can cause shortness of breath

RespiratoryCardiovascularSystemic
COPD, pulmonary embolism, lung cancer, asthma, pulmonary fibrosis, pneumothorax, lower respiratory tract infection Heart failure, anaemia, acute coronary syndrome, hyperlipidaemia, CVA/stroke, atrial fibrillation Renal/liver failure, pulmonary oedema, liver cirrhosis, gastric/duodenal ulcer

What is pneumothorax?

Also known as a collapsed lung — air leaks from the lung and fills the space between the lung and chest wall, putting pressure on the lung and making it difficult to expand when inhaling.

How can renal failure lead to shortness of breath?

  • Impaired kidney function → reduced urine production and fluid retention
  • Excess fluid accumulates in the body, including the lungs → pulmonary oedema (fluid in alveolar spaces)
  • Kidneys fail to excrete H⁺ and reabsorb bicarbonate → metabolic acidosis; the body compensates by increasing respiratory rate (Kussmaul breathing) to expel CO₂

How can liver failure lead to shortness of breath?

  • Liver failure (esp. from cirrhosis) → portal hypertension
  • Fluid accumulates in the abdominal cavity (ascites), increasing intra-abdominal pressure
  • The diaphragm's movement is restricted, reducing lung expansion → a restrictive lung pattern

How can COPD decrease quality of life? (the spiral effect)

  • Being diagnosed with COPD can be a shock; a COPD exacerbation reduces lung function
  • Decreased lung function → decreased physical activity (reduced aerobic capacity)
  • This can decrease mental health → decreased quality of life

3 Chest X-ray Interpretation

The ABCDE approach to viewing an X-ray

  • Airways — assess the trachea's position (should be midline)
  • Breathing — assess lungs and pleural spaces; examine for asymmetry, opacities, or hyperlucency
  • Circulation — assess mediastinum, heart and great vessels
  • Disability — focus on the diaphragm (right hemidiaphragm normally higher than left due to the liver)
  • Everything else — bones, soft tissue, lines and tubes

What is consolidation on an X-ray, and its causes?

  • A region where normal air-filled alveoli are replaced by something denser (fluid, pus, blood, or cells), appearing more opaque (whiter) than normal (black) lung
  • Infectious: pneumonia (most common — pus/inflammatory fluid fills the alveoli)
  • Non-infectious: pulmonary oedema (heart failure), haemorrhage, aspiration, tumour

Features expected on a COPD X-ray

  • Hyperinflation of the lungs (loss of elastic recoil + air trapping)
  • Flattened diaphragm (secondary to lung inflation)
  • Increased darkness within the lungs (more air from hyperinflation)
  • Pleural effusion (only in extreme cases)

Pleural effusion and the silhouette sign

  • Pleural effusion: accumulation of excessive fluid in the pleural space → reduced transparency and increased opacity; the costophrenic angle may be obscured. Fluid appears greyish.
  • Silhouette sign: a radiological sign where a disease process (mass, pneumonia, consolidation) obscures the normal outline of an adjacent structure (usually heart or diaphragm), helping localise pathology

4 Acid–Base Balance

The four acid–base disturbances

DisturbanceDefinition
Respiratory acidosispH < 7.35, secondary to hypercapnia from respiratory failure
Metabolic acidosispH < 7.35, secondary to reduced ability of kidneys to excrete H⁺ or reabsorb HCO₃⁻
Respiratory alkalosispH > 7.45, secondary to hypocapnia from respiratory failure
Metabolic alkalosispH > 7.45, secondary to reduced ability of kidneys to excrete HCO₃⁻ or reabsorb H⁺

Intercalated cells and renal compensation

  • Intercalated cells control the acid–base balance of the blood. Two types: alpha and beta
  • Alpha intercalated cells (stop acidosis): prevent H⁺ movement into blood; increase blood pH via HCO₃⁻ reabsorption (HCO₃⁻/Cl⁻ antiporter) and H⁺ secretion (H⁺ ATPase)
  • Beta intercalated cells (stop alkalosis): allow H⁺ movement into blood; decrease blood pH via H⁺ reabsorption and HCO₃⁻ secretion
  • The kidneys are the organ involved in resolving acidosis/alkalosis

The anion gap

  • A calculated value reflecting the difference between the main cations (Na⁺, K⁺) and anions (Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻) in plasma, representing the unmeasured anions
  • Used to help determine the underlying cause of metabolic acidosis — differentiating accumulation of organic acids vs loss of bicarbonate
  • Normally there are slightly more unmeasured anions than cations, giving a normal anion gap
  • Two main causes of metabolic acidosis: increased H⁺ ions, reduced HCO₃⁻ ions

5 Respiratory Failure

Type 1 vs Type 2 respiratory failure

Blood gasesLow O₂ (hypoxemia) with normal or low CO₂ (normocapnia/hypocapnia)Elevated CO₂ (hypercapnia), often with low O₂ (hypoxemia)
Primary problemOxygenation / gas exchange failureVentilation failure (failure to move sufficient air in/out of alveoli)
Usual causeV/Q mismatch or shuntingFailure to exchange/remove CO₂

Note Because CO₂ diffuses across the alveolar–capillary membrane much more efficiently than O₂, it takes significant ventilation dysfunction for hypercapnia to be present.

Causes of Type 1 and Type 2 respiratory failure

  • Type 1: pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, pulmonary embolism, pulmonary fibrosis, ARDS, aspiration
  • Type 2: reduced respiratory drive, upper airway obstruction, late severe acute asthma, COPD, peripheral neuromuscular disease, flail chest injury, exhaustion

6 The Haldane Effect & O₂ Targets in COPD

Why must oxygen saturation be controlled differently in COPD?

  • Mainly due to the Haldane effect
  • COPD patients are at high risk of oxygen-induced hypercapnia
  • Target SpO₂ in COPD: 88–92% (vs >96% in healthy individuals)

Hypoxic drive

  • In healthy individuals, breathing is primarily driven by CO₂ levels — high CO₂ stimulates the respiratory centre to increase rate and depth
  • In some COPD patients, chronic CO₂ retention desensitises the respiratory centre to high CO₂; breathing then relies on O₂ levels — the hypoxic drive
  • Giving too much oxygen can suppress the hypoxic drive, reducing respiratory rate and causing dangerous CO₂ buildup (hypercapnic respiratory failure)

What is the Haldane effect?

  • The ability of deoxyhaemoglobin to carry more CO₂ than oxyhaemoglobin
  • Important when restoring O₂ saturation in obstructive lung disease: if saturation is too high, it reduces CO₂ binding to haemoglobin
  • This leads to oxygen-induced hypercapnia via reduced CO₂ clearance, as less CO₂ leaves the lungs

7 V/Q Matching & Pulmonary Hypertension

Why does vasoconstriction occur in the lungs during hypoxia?

  • A compensatory mechanism redirecting blood to well-oxygenated areas of the body
  • Well-oxygenated lung areas do not vasoconstrict, but poorly oxygenated areas do — increasing gas exchange efficiency
  • When prolonged, this can lead to pulmonary hypertension (common in COPD where alveoli are frequently poorly ventilated)
  • Based on the principle of ventilation–perfusion (V/Q) matching

Consequences of pulmonary hypertension

  • Right ventricular hypertrophy — the high-pressure system in the pulmonary arteries forces the right ventricle to contract harder and pump blood at higher pressure than normal

Hydrostatic vs oncotic pressure in capillaries

  • Arterial end: hydrostatic pressure (~30–40 mmHg) exceeds oncotic pressure (~25 mmHg) → favours filtration of fluid out of the capillary
  • Venous end: hydrostatic pressure decreases (~10–15 mmHg), oncotic pressure dominates → fluid reabsorption back into the capillary

8 Managing Comorbidities

How can corticosteroids affect management of other conditions?

  • Diabetes: worsen it by promoting gluconeogenesis, causing insulin resistance, and reducing glucose uptake (Cushingoid effects); may need dose adjustments of anti-diabetic medications
  • Hypertension: cortisol-like effects can raise blood pressure — not ideal in hypertensive patients
  • Depression: acute steroids can provoke anxiety; a mental state examination can monitor the condition

How can chronic kidney disease (CKD) affect management of COPD?

  • The acidosis common in COPD (from reduced CO₂ clearance) is less likely to be resolved well by the kidneys due to impaired compensatory mechanisms
  • This means more focus is needed on managing electrolyte imbalances than usual
